## 1. Overview

- Models can be simplifications of the world, but models can also take the form of analogies or, alternatively, the models themselves may be virtual worlds constructed for exploring ideas and summarizing opinions
- Models are also valuable in that they can make explicit the conditions required for a particular outcome.
- Models can also reveal the conditions under which intuitive conclusions are likely to hold true

## 2. 3 Ways to Build Models

- Embodiment approach

  - This approach builds a model that includes important parts, while unnecessary dimensions and attributes are either stripped away or considered together.
  - Examples
    - Model of an ecological swamp
    - Models of legislatures and transportation systems
    - Climate models
    - Brain models
    ---
  - More emphasis on realism
- **analogy approach

  - Analogies and abstractions of reality
  - Examples
    - Spread of criminal behavior: spread of infectious diseases
    - Choice of political position: choice on a continuous left-right line segment
    ---.
  - Commitment to portraying the nature of a process, system, or phenomenon
- **alternative realization approach (alternative reality approach)

  - Intentionally not characterizing or portraying reality
  - Using such models to explore a wide range of possibilities
  - Allows us to discover general conclusions that apply beyond the physical and social worlds.
  - It helps us to better understand the implications of real-world constraints.
- Any model must be easy to handle and easy to communicate.
- Any model must be manageable and easy to communicate. We can encode the model in a formal language.
- We can encode the model in a formal language **When describing the model, we can't just throw out things like beliefs or preferences without giving a formal description**.

  - Beliefs: can be expressed as a series of events or a prior probability distribution
  - Preferences: can be identified in a variety of ways (e.g., expressed as a ranking of a set of alternatives or a mathematical function)
- Ease of handling is the property of being suitable for analysis
- Occam's Razor principle: Plurality must never be posited without necessity**.
- Einstein further elaborated the principle of Occam's Razor as follows: **Things should be kept as simple as possible, but not oversimplified** **When faced with a problem that is difficult to deal with by analytic methods, the principle of Occam's Razor should be applied to it.
- When faced with problems that are difficult to deal with analytically, we can also turn to computational methods, which allow us to build fine-grained models with many changing components, regardless of analytic ease of handling.

## 3. 7 Uses of Models (REDCAPE)

- **Reasoning**: recognizing conditions and inferring logical implications

  - When constructing a model, start by identifying the most important actors (actors), entities, and associated characteristics
  - Then, describing how these components interact and coalesce, we are able to deduce something and explain what the reason is
  - Although, what can be deduced depends on the assumptions, there is more to discover through modeling than just **repeat (tautology) **
  - We can rarely determine the full impact of a hypothesis based on testing alone, we need formal logic

    - Logic can also reveal impossibilities and possibilities
  - Logic can reveal paradoxes
  - Logic can reveal mathematical relationships
  - Logic reveals the conditional nature of truth
  - Using models we can draw a line between when they should be used and when they should not be used
  - Conclusions drawn from models are always in the form of conditional judgments
  - In models, theorems can be proved under given assumptions
- Explain**: to provide a (testable) explanation of an empirical phenomenon.

  - Models provide clear logical explanations for empirical phenomena
  - Models can explain changes in **point values** and **point values**.
  - Three properties that most social phenomena do not have

    - Social actors are heterogeneous
    - Interactions occur within small groups
    - Actors do not follow fixed rules
  - Models can explain anything
  - Model-based explanations must include formal assumptions and explicit causal chains that are confronted with data
- **Design**: choosing characteristics of institutions, policies and rules

  - Models can help design by providing a framework
  - Only within an appropriate framework can we consider the implications of different choices
- **Communicate**: linking knowledge and understanding

  - Models require formal definitions of relevant features and their relationships, which allow us to communicate precisely
  - A thought that cannot be communicated is like a tree submerged in the forest, no one will notice it
- **Action (Act)**: guides policy choices and strategic action

  - Good actions require good models
  - Models that guide actions usually rely on data, but not all models rely on data
  - Most policy models require the use of mathematical formulas, but not always.
- Most policy models require the use of mathematical formulas, but not always. **Predict**: numerical and categorical predictions of future and unknown phenomena.

  - Models have long been used to predict
  - Models can be used to predict both specific individual events and general trends
  - Some models can be used to predict. But don't necessarily explain anything
  - Some models have strong explanatory power, but little value in prediction
- Explore**: analyze and explore possibilities and hypotheses.

  - Models can be used to explore intuition
  - There are many hypotheses that we can formulate, and all of them can be explored with models.
  - We can use models to explore situations that don't occur in the real world.
  - Exploration sometimes involves cross-domain comparisons of common hypotheses
