## 1. Overview

- **Core question: how to model people? **
- Making assumptions about people

  - What are the goals?
  - Is it an egoist or an altruist?
  - What are the possible actions?
  - How do you choose the actions you want to take (do you have a choice)?
  ---
- Modeling people as **rule-based actors**, or **rational actors

  - Rule-based actor
    - In this set, we consider people who act based on simple fixed rules as well as people who act based on adaptive forms of rules
- How we model people depends ultimately on the context of the problem and the goals we want to achieve.
- Challenges in modeling people

  - People are diverse
  - People are susceptible to social influences
  - People are fallible

## 2. Challenges to human modeling ##

- Models require low-dimensional representations, but people are inherently difficult to characterize simply
- Diversity

  - People change their behavior based on past experiences, are subjective, and dominate purposeful life
- Susceptible to social influences

  - People differ in their preferences, ability to act, formation of social networks, altruistic tendencies, and cognitive resources allocated to different actions
- Prone to error

  - Whether or not the mistakes people make cancel each other out depends on the specific situation
    - Errors due to \*\*cognitive attachment\*\*\*deficits may be random and independent
    - Errors due to cognitive biases may be systematic and correlated
- Purposeful

  - Difficult to accurately assess people's goals and objectives
- Adaptability

  - People are willing to live in an "integrated" community
- Ownership

  - People have the ability to take action, to change their behavior, and to learn.

## 3. The Rational Behavior Model

- Assumes that people make optimal choices given a payoff or utility function
- **Rational actor model***

  - Individual actor's preferences are represented by a mathematical form of utility or benefit function defined over a set of possible actions
  - The individual actor first acts to maximize the value of the function
  - In a game, such choices may require beliefs about the behavior of other game participants

### 3.1 Loss aversion and hyperbolic discounting

- We can ignore the underlying ratios when making probability calculations, give too much weight to certainties, and also exhibit loss aversion
- Two ways of thinking

  - Think fast.
    - Fast thinking is more susceptible to biases
  - Slow thinking.
    - Slow thinking is used to minimize biases.
- More realistic models may require more complex math
- Loss aversion

  - When faced with gains, people are risk averse.
  - In the face of loss, people are risk-averse.
  - **prospect theory***
- hyperbolic discounting

  - People discount more strongly for the near term

### 3.2 Rule-based modeling

- Optimization-based models: assume that people maximize a utility or benefit function
- Rule-based models: assume specific behaviors
- Behavioral rules can be either fixed or adaptive (**Elfaroux model**)
  - Fixed rules: the same algorithm is always applied
    - Can be used as a lower bound for human cognitive ability
  - Adaptive rules: can switch between a range of behaviors, evolve new behaviors or copy others
- Micro-macro cycle
  - Micro level: individuals act on rules, creating macro level phenomena
  - Macro level: feedback back to micro level rules

## 4. what results the model produces

- How smart should an actor be?

  - Tension arises when our models only produce inefficient or even poor macro results
  - Anyone who underperforms can do better
  - People need to be able to make better rules of action
- What kind of results does the model produce?

  - The 4 categories of outcomes
    - Balanced
    - Balanced
      - Can create a stable environment
      - People are expected to learn and no one will consistently take sub-optimal action
    - Randomness
      - Individuals may not learn anything
    - Complexity
      - We can assume that people can continue to adapt to new rules, but we cannot assume that they can choose optimal rules
      - Complexity of macro phenomena can make optimal responses seem implausible
  - The class of outcomes will determine how much weight we give to the argument that humans should learn to achieve equilibrium
- If the model produces equilibria (simple cycles) and the equilibrium is inconsistent with optimal behavior, that means our model is logically flawed
- If people can act better, they should be able to figure it out and they should learn
- In order to reach equilibrium, we don't need to assume optimal behavior
- If the adaptive rules that allow any action to be taken produce an equilibrium, then that equilibrium must be consistent with the behavior of the actor committed to optimization
- If these adaptive rules create complexity, then the behavior of the actor is not necessarily optimal
- Optimal behavior may be an unrealistic assumption, especially in complex situations
- If a system produces stable outcomes and a person can act better, then that person is likely to find that better option
- **Lucasian Critique**: people react to any measure or standard in a way that makes them less efficient

  - Changes in policy or environment may elicit behavioral responses from those affected
  - Models estimated using past behavioral data will be inaccurate
  - Models must take into account the fact that people react to policy and environmental changes
